How Quee Wah v Heng Joke Wah

How Quee Wah v Heng Joke Wah
[2022] SGDC 284

Case Number:District Court Suit No 685 of 2021, District Court Appeal No 37 of 2022
Decision Date:28 November 2022
Tribunal/Court:District Court
Coram: Jonathan Toh Jun Hian
Counsel Name(s): Ng Boon Gan (VanillaLaw LLC) for the plaintiff; Defendant in person.
Parties: How Quee Wah — Heng Joke Wah

Trusts – Resulting Trust – Whether presumption of resulting trust arose

[LawNet Editorial Note: An appeal to this decision has been filed in HC/DCA 37/2022.]

28 November 2022

District Judge Jonathan Toh Jun Hian:

Introduction

1       The plaintiff is an elderly lady who lived with her deceased brother in a Housing and Development Board (“HDB”) flat. Her brother was married to the defendant. After he died, the defendant evicted the plaintiff from the flat. The plaintiff sued for a declaration that the defendant held 56.91% of the flat on trust for her as a result of a purchase price resulting trust.

2       The plaintiff’s pleaded case and evidence was that she made instalment payments for the flat pursuant to a verbal agreement between her and her brother: that in exchange for the plaintiff paying the remainder of the flat’s purchase price, the plaintiff and her mother would be allowed to reside in the flat.

3       I dismissed her claim as there was no scope for a purchase price resulting trust to arise. The plaintiff simply made instalment payments to HDB pursuant to an agreement that allowed her and her mother to reside in the flat. This was nothing more than a license agreement.

4       The plaintiff has appealed and I set out my full grounds of decision.

The Facts

5       In 1974, the plaintiff’s deceased brother (the “Deceased”) purchased a HDB flat at Block 30 Marine Crescent #XXX, Singapore 440030 (the “Flat”) from HDB for the price of $13,996. The Deceased paid $6,030.71 of the purchase price.

6       Shortly after purchasing the Flat and making the initial payment, the Deceased became unemployed. The plaintiff’s pleaded case was that she entered into a verbal agreement with the Deceased: in exchange for paying the remainder of the purchase price of the Flat, the plaintiff and their mother would be allowed to reside in the Flat.[note: 1]

7       It was not disputed that the plaintiff made all subsequent instalment payments to HDB for the Flat, amounting to $7,965.29. The last payment was made on 31 January 1996.[note: 2]

8       On 12 June 2004, the Deceased and the defendant married. However, the defendant never moved into the Flat to live with the Deceased. Her case was that the plaintiff did not like her and did not want the Deceased to marry her. The Deceased told the defendant not to quarrel with the plaintiff. The defendant spent each day in the flat with the Deceased but would leave in the evenings.[note: 3] The plaintiff did not say anything to contradict these facts.

9       The Deceased passed away on 12 August 2019 and the defendant was made administratrix of the estate pursuant to letters of administration granted under FC/P 5212/2019 on or around 11 November 2019.[note: 4]

10     On 24 November 2020, the defendant obtained legal ownership of the Flat as the sole beneficiary of the Deceased’s estate.[note: 5] On 20 June 2021, the defendant evicted the plaintiff from the Flat.[note: 6]

Did the presumption of a purchase price resulting trust arise?

11     The plaintiff’s claim is for a purchase price resulting trust over the Flat. The critical part of her Statement of Claim describes specifically why she paid the balance purchase price of the Flat:[note: 7]

The Plaintiff paid the sum of $7,956.29 on a verbal agreement with the Deceased, made in or around 1974, that in exchange for paying the remainder of the purchase price of the Property, the Plaintiff and their mother would be allowed to reside in the Property.

12     In her AEIC, she explained it as follows:[note: 8]

“Shortly after applying to purchase the Property and paying $6,030.71 of the purchase price, my brother became unemployed… since my mother and I were also residing in the Property, I spoke to my brother and we agreed that I would to (sic) pay for the remainder of the purchase price, so that we could continue residing at the Property…”

13     The Court of Appeal in Su Emmanuel v Emmanuel Priya Ethel Anne [2016] 3 SLR 1222 (“Su Emmanuel”) explained the nature of the purchase price resulting trust. The following principles are relevant to this case.

(a)     A presumption of resulting trust arises when a person makes a voluntary payment to another person or paid (wholly or in part) for the purchase of property which vested in the other person (Su Emmanuel at [77]).

(b)     The doctrinal basis for this presumption is that the law does not readily infer an intention by the payor of the purchase price to benefit the recipient of the property (Su Emmanuel at [78]).

(c)     This lack of intention to benefit the recipient is inferred. As such, the presumption of resulting trust will not be called in aid when the evidence that is before the court adequately reveals the true intention of the transferor. (Su Emmanuel at [79]).

(d)     The focus is on the parties’ intentions at the time the property was acquired as the resulting trust crystallises at the time the property is acquired (Su Emmanuel at [87]).

14     In Su Emmanuel, the payor purchased 49% of a property from the recipient for a specific price. Subsequently, the property was refinanced and a fresh mortgage was entered into between the payor, the recipient, and another party. The Court of Appeal found that at the time this mortgage was entered into, parties intended to contribute mortgage payments in accordance with their respective share. However, the payor made all of the subsequent payments. The Court of Appeal found that the subsequent mortgage payments did not give rise to a presumption of resulting trust because the payor only purchased 49% of the Property from the recipient. As such, their actual intentions on the payor’s share of the property were proved and there was no room for a presumption of resulting trust to arise. As the co-owners had contributed to mortgage payments differently from their intended share, the court applied the concept of equitable accounting to reimburse the payor for the excess sums that she paid.

15     I turn now to the present case. In my view, on the plaintiff’s own pleaded case and evidence, the plaintiff and the deceased’s intentions relating to the purchase of the Flat and the plaintiff’s payment of the balance purchase price are clear. It was pursuant to an agreement between the plaintiff and the Deceased for the plaintiff and her mother to be allowed to reside in the Flat. As such, there is no room for the presumption of resulting trust. I explain.

16     When the Flat was first acquired, the Deceased purchased the property on his own. There was no intention on the plaintiff’s part to contribute to the purchase price.[note: 9] It was only after the Deceased acquired the property and became unemployed that he agreed with the plaintiff for her to pay the remainder of the purchase price.[note: 10]

17     In this respect, the content of this verbal agreement as pleaded by the plaintiff is both crucial and fatal to her claim for a purchase price resulting trust. The parties’ intentions are spelt out in the pleaded agreement:[note: 11] In exchange for paying the remainder of the purchase price of the Flat, the plaintiff and their mother were allowed to reside in the Flat. This was an agreement premised on a mutual exchange of obligations. The plaintiff was obliged to pay the remainder of the purchase price and the Deceased was obliged to allow the plaintiff and their mother to reside in the Flat. In other words, this was nothing more than a license agreement.

18     Applying Su Emmanuel at [79] (see [13(c)] above), the presumption of a resulting trust cannot apply in this case to infer the plaintiff’s lack of intention to benefit the Deceased. The plaintiff has expressly pleaded and given evidence that she made payment of the balance purchase price pursuant to an agreement for her and her mother to be allowed to reside in the Flat. As such, there is no room left for the presumption of resulting trust to arise. The payment is simply consideration for her being allowed to reside in the Flat, and does not give the plaintiff an interest in the Flat.

19     For the same reason, equitable accounting for excess mortgage payments is not available in this case. The plaintiff did not make any payments on behalf of the Deceased in excess of their common understanding of what their obligations were. She simply paid the balance purchase price for the Flat pursuant to the pleaded agreement.

20     This is not an issue that should have caught the plaintiff by surprise. At the commencement of trial, I specifically raised the issue of whether the plaintiff’s pleaded agreement was consistent with her claim for a purchase price resulting trust. Plaintiff’s counsel indicated that he would address the issue in closing submissions, but he did not do so.[note: 12]

21     In the circumstances, I dismissed the plaintiff’s claim for a purchase price resulting trust. As the plaintiff was legally aided and the defendant was acting in person, I made no orders as to costs.
