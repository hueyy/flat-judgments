4       I sentenced each of the accused persons to a total sentence of 33 months imprisonment. The accused persons are dissatisfied with the sentences imposed and have both filed appeals.

Facts

5       In early 2021, Zhang was introduced to a person known to him as ‘Ah Xiang’, who was purportedly in the business of online gaming and soccer betting. Ah Xiang asked Zhang if he would use his bank account to receive and transfer out funds to other bank accounts. Zhang was told that the funds to be received and transferred would amount to tens of thousands each day. Zhang told Ah Xiang that he was not prepared to use his own bank account for these transfers, as he thought his account might be frozen if there were large amounts of money flowing in and out.

6       Ah Xiang knew that Zhang worked at an agency for foreign workers and suggested that Zhang use the bank accounts of foreign workers to conduct the transfers of funds instead. He told Zhang that he would be paid S$200 in commission for every S$10,000 that he helped receive into a bank account.

7       Zhang discussed Ah Xiang’s proposal with his brother-in-law, Yin. Zhang and Yin agreed that they would use the bank accounts of foreign workers who were leaving Singapore to conduct the inward receipt and outward transfer of funds as directed by Ah Xiang. Zhang and Yin started collecting ATM cards and internet banking credentials for bank accounts belonging to foreign workers who were leaving Singapore and shared the details of these bank accounts with Ah Xiang via Telegram.

8       Ah Xiang would alert Zhang and Yin via a Telegram chat group each time transfers of funds were made into the bank accounts. On the three 3 proceeded charges, the amounts that were received in the bank accounts controlled by Zhang and Yin are as follows:

9       After the funds were received in the bank accounts, either Zhang or Yin (who both had control over the bank accounts) would withdraw the monies received via ATM machines in cash and then make arrangements to transfer the monies to cryptocurrency wallets or foreign bank accounts, in accordance with Ah Xiang’s instructions.

10     Investigations revealed that the sums received in the bank accounts were in fact the proceeds of crimes, specifically cheating offences under section 420 of the Penal Code. Both Zhang and Yin had reasonable grounds to believe that the monies that they received in the bank accounts directly represented, in whole, another person’s benefits of criminal conduct.

11     Zhang and Yin’s activities came to light after they were arrested on 20 June 2021 after being stopped in at a police roadblock. Zhang was the driver of the vehicle, while Yin was the passenger. In the vehicle, the police officers found cash totalling S$406,850 and 71 ATM bank cards. The accused persons were unable to provide a satisfactory explanation for why they were in possession of the cash and ATM cards. This formed the subject of the TIC charge under section 35 of the Miscellaneous Offences Act that each accused person faced.

Antecedents

12     Both Zhang and Yin were traced with antecedents that were not relevant to their present case. They each had a previous conviction for drink driving. Zhang was previously also placed on drug supervision for the consumption of methamphetamine and amphetamine.

Prosecution’s sentencing submission

13     In seeking a global sentence of 36 months’ imprisonment for each accused person, the Prosecution highlighted the following offence-specific aggravating factors, which the High Court in Huang Ying-Chun v Public Prosecutor [2019] 3 SLR 606 (“Huang Ying-Chun”) had set out as sentencing considerations for offences under section 44(1)(a) CDSA, which the Prosecution argued can be applied to section 47 CDSA offences:

(a)     The amount of criminal proceeds laundered was very large. Each of them faced charges for conspiring to transfer a total of $912,129.70 in criminal proceeds, which they first received in the bank accounts and then transferred to foreign bank accounts and cryptocurrency wallets.

(b)     There was a transnational element involved in the offences, given the transfer of the criminal proceeds to foreign bank accounts.

(c)     Premeditation and sophistication in the criminal enterprise, which involved using the bank accounts of foreign nationals who were leaving Singapore to receive criminal proceedings.

(d)     The accused persons offended over a significant period of time from 3 May 2021 to 20 June 2021, stopping only when they were arrested on 20 June 2021.

(e)     The accused persons were motivated by the prospect of large rewards. Zhang received $200 in commission for every $10,000 that was received, which he shared equally with Yin. For the $912,129.70 that was received in total by the accused person, they would thus have received $18,242.60 in commission.

(f)     The accused persons each face three TIC charges.

14     The Prosecution noted that the accused persons had not made any restitution.

15     The Prosecution cited precedent cases involving offences under section 47 of the CDSA. The offenders in those cases were sentenced to varying imprisonment terms. The table setting out the details of the precedents that the Prosecution tendered to Court is at Annex A.

Defence’s sentencing submission and mitigation

16     The Defence sought a global sentence of 18 to 24 months’ imprisonment against each accused person. In their submissions, they urged the Court to consider the following factors, which were also based on the sentencing factors set out by the High Court in Huang Ying-Chun

(a)     While the total amount involved across all the CDSA charges was $912,129.70, the amount involved in the proceeded charges was $602,461.70 and the 2 charges that the Prosecution was seeking consecutive sentences on involved $498,558.00. The Defence argued that the Court ought to primarily focus on the proceeded charges for the purposes of sentencing, and then look at the amount in the TIC charges after that.

(b)     The accused persons had no knowledge as to the offence-specific factors going towards harm as set out in Huang Ying-Chun, such as involvement of a syndicate, seriousness of the predicate offence and harm done to the confidence in public administration, as the accused persons were involved into the receiving and transferring of the monies and knew nothing else.

(c)     The collecting of ATM cards and bank account details from foreigners was not a sophisticated scheme that involved a high degree of planning and premeditation. In their written submissions, the Defence asserted that “practically anyone could have thought of such a scheme”.

(d)     The period of time that accused persons engaged in their criminal activity was not a long period, being 49 days.

(e)     The accused persons did not have direct knowledge of the underlying predicate offences and were only involved in receiving and transferring the monies.

(f)     The accused persons were not in Singapore for the sole purpose of committing the offences but had either been studying or working in Singapore for a long time.

(g)     The accused persons did not have any relevant antecedents, had cooperated with the authorities and pleaded guilty at the earliest opportunity.

17     The Defence also cited a few precedents, including the case of Public Prosecutor v Lee Jun Xiao [2022] SGDC 146 (“Lee Jun Xiao”). It submitted that the indicative sentencing ranges proposed by the District Court in Lee Jun Xiao could be applied in the present case:

Reasons for the sentences imposed

Punishment provisions

19     Pursuant to section 47(6)(a) of the CDSA, the offence under section 47(2)(b) of the CDSA is punishable with a fine not exceeding $500,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 10 years or to both.

20     Two of the proceeded charges were amalgamated pursuant to section 124(2) of the CPC, and accordingly section 124(8)(a)(i) of the CPC applied such that the Court may sentence the accused persons to twice the amount of punishment to which they would otherwise have been liable for the offence.

Sentencing considerations

21     The Prosecution submitted that they had assessed the culpability of both accused persons to be similar and therefore sought the same sentences against them. The Defence similarly proceeded with their submissions on the basis that both accused persons should receive the same sentences and did not seek to distinguish their roles or culpability in any way. Based on the Statement of Facts, while it was Zhang who spoke with Ah Xiang at the outset, he subsequently discussed the plan with Yin and they both agreed to accept Ah Xiang’s offer and proceeded with the plan. As such, I did not see any basis to distinguish their roles in a manner that would warrant different sentences to be imposed on each of them.

22     I agreed with the Prosecution’s submission that general deterrence is the dominant sentencing consideration for cases involving offences under the CDSA. As the High Court in Ang Jeanette v Public Prosecutor [2011] 4 SLR 1 noted at [73], there is a strong need to deter would-be money launders particularly given Singapore’s standing as a major international financial centre in the region.

23     Both parties cited the case of Huang Ying-Chun as a case from which sentencing guidance for this present case can be derived. While Huang Ying-Chun concerned offences under section 44(1)(a) of the CDSA, which is for assisting another to retain benefits from criminal conduct, parties were of the view that the sentencing factors for a section 44(1)(a) offence as set out by the High Court can be applied to offences under section 47(2)(b) of the CDSA.

24     I agreed with parties that the sentencing considerations set out in Huang Ying-Chun are a helpful reference for the sentencing of offences under section 47(2)(b) of the CDSA given that both offences involve the facilitation of the benefits of a third-party’s criminal conduct. Using these sentencing considerations as a reference, I considered the following factors in determining the appropriate sentences to be imposed:

(a)      The amount involved. One of the primary aggravating factors in this case was the substantial amount of funds involved in the charges. $912,129.70 is a very significant amount of money. On the proceeded charges alone, the amount involved stands at S$602,461.70.

(b)      Transnational element. There was clearly a transnational element to the offences, given that the accused persons transferred the funds received to foreign bank accounts.

(c)      Degree of planning and premeditation, and the level of sophistication of the scheme. I agreed with the Prosecution that the offences were clearly premeditated and involved a level of sophistication. In my view, the Defence’s position that this was a scheme that “practically anyone could have thought of” unfairly downplayed the nature of the scheme. The plan to use the bank accounts of foreign nationals who were leaving Singapore was one designed to mitigate the risk of detection, which Zhang was clearly alive to.

(d)      Duration of offending. The offences were committed over a period of 49 days. The Defence argued that this was not a long period of time, as the Prosecution had characterised it. 49 days is indeed not a very long period, but it is clear on this fact that the accused persons had not engaged in a one-off or even brief bout of criminal activity. They were persistent and prolific in their criminal conduct in this period, receiving $912,129.70 in the bank accounts that they controlled over this period of 49 days, stopping only after they were arrested at the roadblock.

(e)      The offender’s knowledge of the underlying predicate offence. The Defence sought to emphasise that the accused persons were not aware of that the funds were proceeds of cheating offences but instead proceeds from illegal online gambling. I agreed with the Defence that there is indeed room to distinguish between an offender who has direct knowledge of the underlying criminal conduct and one who only had reasonable grounds to believe that he was facilitating a third party’s benefits of criminal conduct. That said, even by the accused persons own account, they were never under any illusion (however flimsy) that they were dealing were untainted funds, since they were under the impression that these were the proceeds from illegal online gambling.

(f)      The prospect of reward. Both accused persons had engaged in this criminal enterprise in anticipation of the financial benefits that they would obtain in the form of commissions, which would have amounted to a significant amount based on the agreed rate of $200 per $10,000 received in the bank accounts.

25     Turning to the offender-specific factors, I had regard to the fact that the accused persons had elected to plead guilty to the charges, which I accepted should be accorded mitigating weight.

26     At the hearing, Defence Counsel informed me that the accused persons did not intend to lay claim to the sum of $406,850 that had been seized from them, which formed the subject matter of the TIC charges under section 35 of the Miscellaneous Offences Act. Defence Counsel argued that this amount of money ought to be considered as partial recovery of the funds involved in the case that could be returned to victims.

27     I did not think that much credit could be given for the $406,850 that the accused persons were intending not to lay claim to. This amount was seized from the accused persons when they were caught at a police roadblock, as opposed to having been voluntarily surrendered or paid in restitution. In fact, as the charges against the accused persons make plain, the accused persons were not able to give a satisfactory explanation of why this amount of cash was found in their possession. As the Prosecution pointed out, the sum of $912,129.70 that formed the subject matter of the CDSA charges had been transferred into the foreign bank accounts by the accused persons and did not overlap with the $406,850 in cash that was seized from the accused persons.

Sentences imposed

28     In determining the sentences to be imposed on the accused persons, I considered the precedents that parties had referred to in submissions.

29     Both parties referred to the case of Public Prosecutor v Bahurudeen Kuthpudeen [2018] SGDC 260 as a useful reference point for the third proceeded charges against the accused persons, i.e. the charges involving the amount of $437,558. In that case, an individual sentence of 24 months’ imprisonment was imposed for a charge under section 47(1)(b) of the CDSA involving a sum of $469,900.

30     The charge in Bahurudeen involved the removal of the offender’s proceeds of his own offence under section 408 of the Penal Code, which meant that the offender did have direct knowledge of the underlying predicate offence. I accept that this can be distinguished from a situation where the offenders do not have direct knowledge of the underlying predicate offence. However, I note that while the accused persons in this case did not have direct knowledge of the underlying predicate offence, this was not a case where they had been given some flimsy cover story with red flags that they ignored – they were never under any illusion that these were untainted funds. As Defence Counsel emphasised several times during the hearing, the accused persons had acted under the impression that they were dealing in the proceeds of illegal online gambling.

31     I also noted that the offender in Bahurudeen engaged in a single act of removing his proceeds of crime from Singapore by carrying a sum of cash with him as he left Singapore via the Woodlands Checkpoint. In comparison, the third proceeded charges against the accused persons were amalgamated charges that covered conduct over a period of 10 days. The means by which the funds were transferred were also more complex than that in Bahurudeen. There was also the fact that the accused persons in this case faced additional TIC charges for similar offences.

32     On a balance of these factors, in my view, the conduct of the accused persons on this charge was more aggravated than that of the offender in Bahurudeen and so while the amounts involved in the third proceeded charges and Bahurudeen were similar, I was of the view that a higher sentence of 28 months’ imprisonment against each accused person was appropriate on the third proceeded charges.

33     As for the first and second proceeded charges involving the sums of $61,000 and $103,903.70, I sentenced the accused persons to 5 months’ imprisonment and 9 months’ imprisonment respectively. In doing so, I had regard to the precedents cited by the Prosecution, Public Prosecutor v Ng Koon Lay [2020] SGDC 196 and Public Prosecutor v Zulkiffili Bin Radi [2016] SGDC 145, where the offenders faced charges under section 47(2)(b) of the CDSA involving similar amounts as the first and second proceeded charges. I also noted that the sentences that I had determined were not out of step with the indicative sentencing ranges derived by the District Court in Lee Jun Xiao, which had been cited by the Defence.

34     The Defence also sought to rely on the case of Public Prosecutor v Nordiah Bte Bakar, an unreported decision that was referred to in Public Prosecutor v Ambrose Dionysius [2018] SGDC 35. Given that no grounds of decision were available, the precedential value of this case was therefore limited, and I did not have significant regard for it.

Consecutive sentences

35     I ordered the sentences on the first and third proceeded charges to run consecutively. This was the position that both parties took before me as well.

36     I was of the view that the totality principle had not been offended by the aggregate imprisonment term of 33 months imprisonment. In my view, this was not a crushing sentence in view of the accused persons’ overall offending conduct, their records, and prospects.
