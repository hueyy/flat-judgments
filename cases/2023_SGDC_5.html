District Judge Carol Ling Feng Yong:

Introduction

1       The Accused pleaded guilty to these two charges:

DAC 905563/2022

You, are charged that you, on 6 April 2022, at about 5.03 pm, in the vicinity of Block 175 Boon Lay Drive, Singapore, together with Niswan s/o Thiruchelvam, in furtherance of the common intention of you both, did voluntarily cause grievous hurt to Praveen Raj s/o Chanthiran, by means of instruments for cutting, to wit, by using two bread knives to slash the said Praveen Raj s/o Chanthiran multiple times, and thereby caused him to suffer multiple injuries including a left distal ulna fracture, a left index finger distal phalynx fracture and a right distal humerus fracture, and you have thereby committed an offence punishable under Section 326 read with section 34 of the Penal Code 1871.

DAC 906510/2022

are charged that you, from 1 July 2020 to 30 December 2021, in Singapore, have been a member of an unlawful society, to wit, the ‘Ang Soon Tong’ Triad Society of the ‘Ji It’ Group and you have thereby committed an offence punishable under section 14(3) of the Societies Act (Chapter 311, 2014 Rev Ed).

2       Three other charges (for voluntarily causing grievous hurt with dangerous weapon under section 326 of the Penal Code against another victim, causing hurt under section 323 of the Penal Code and one charge of being a member of an unlawful society under section 14(3) Societies Act) were taken into consideration (TIC) in sentencing.

3       The Accused was 20 years old when he pleaded guilty (Date of Birth: 15 November 2001). The Reformative Training (RT) suitability report called for prior to sentencing found the Accused physically and mentally suitable for reformative training at Level Two intensity i.e. a minimum of 12 months detention in an RT centre. Notwithstanding the recommendation in the RT report, I imposed the following sentences:

DAC 905563/2022 - 36 months' imprisonment and six strokes of the cane.

DAC 906510/2022 - Three months’ imprisonment.

4       The imprisonment terms were ordered to run concurrently. The total sentence was: 36 months’ imprisonment and six strokes of the cane, backdated to 7 April 2022 when he was arrested.

5       This is the Accused’s appeal against the sentence.

The Approach to Sentencing Young Offenders

26     The Accused was under 21 years old at the time of the offences and when he pleaded guilty to the charges.

27     Where young offenders are concerned, the approach to sentencing is well-established. It involves a two-step approach as set out in Public Prosecutor v Mohammad Al-Ansari bin Basri [2008] 1 SLR(R) 449 (“Al-Ansari framework”) and elaborated upon in Public Prosecutor v Koh Wen Jie Boaz (“Boaz”) [2016] 1 SLR 334. The approach was affirmed by the Court of Appeal in Public Prosecutor v ASR [2019] SLR 941 and reiterated in See Li Quan Mendel v Public Prosecutor (“Mendel”) [2020] SGCA 61.

28     As succinctly put in [28] of Boaz:

“It is well established that when a court sentences a youthful offender, it approaches the task in two distinct but related stages (PP v Mohammad Al-Ansari bin Basri [2008] 1 SLR(R) 449 (“PP v Al-Ansari”) at [77]–[78]). At the first stage of the sentencing process, the task for the court is to identify and prioritise the primary sentencing considerations appropriate to the youth in question having regard to all the circumstances including those of the offence. This will then set the parameters for the second stage of the inquiry, which is to select the appropriate sentence that would best meet those sentencing considerations and the priority that the sentencing judge has placed upon the relevant ones.”

29     Having regard to the first stage of the Al-Ansari framework which required the Court to consider all the circumstances of the case when coming to a final determination as to whether rehabilitation remained the primary sentencing consideration[note: 4], I called for an RT report.

Defence’s Mitigation/Submissions

30     The Accused was found physically and mentally fit for reformative training and a Level 2 intensity of rehabilitation was recommended.

31     Defence Counsel urged the Court to impose a term of reformative training on the Accused. In his mitigation, most of which centred on the circumstances surrounding the commission of the section 326 Penal Code offence, Defence Counsel highlighted that the Accused had pleaded guilty, demonstrating genuine remorse for his actions[note: 5]. He had also turned himself in to authorities at the “earliest instance” and co-operated with the police[note: 6]. Explaining the circumstances leading to the offence under section 326 Penal Code, Defence Counsel stated that the Accused had been “instigated” by the victim (referring to Praveen), over a period of time. During one of the many exchanges over the phone in March 2022, Praveen had hurled insults at the Accused’s step-mother who is deaf and dumb and had also used multiple expletives and vulgarities against the Accused during a phone conversation in March 2022. The Accused felt compelled to retaliate[note: 7].

32     Defence Counsel submitted that the Accused’s reformative prospects were “relatively good”[note: 8]. That being so, rehabilitation may not be outweighed by the need for deterrence despite the gravity of the offence committed by the Accused[note: 9]. Further, even in serious offences such as drug-related offences, armed robbery on a public bus or causing hurt by a dangerous weapon, courts have held that rehabilitation remained a dominant sentencing consideration[note: 10].

33     Defence Counsel submitted that the principle of rehabilitation was “arguably diminished but not eclipsed in the sentencing matrix”. From the RT report, it could be seen that the Accused was amenable to reform and his positive attitude and personal circumstances along with a conducive family environment showed that he had “some form of rehabilitative prospects”[note: 11]. Defence Counsel strongly urged the Court to impose a term of RT on the Accused - a sentence which encapsulated the twin principles of rehabilitation and deterrence[note: 12].

Reasons for the Sentence

34     I was unable to accept the submissions of Defence Counsel.

35     The fact that rehabilitation remained a consideration even if not the dominant one (thus making reformative training a viable sentence) was squarely rejected by the High Court in Mendel’s case[note: 13]. As stated in Mendel at [13]:

At the first stage of the Al-Ansari framework, once rehabilitation has been displaced as the dominant sentencing consideration, as for instance in this case by reason of the gravity of the offence and the harm caused, the dominant consideration almost necessarily turns to deterrence. By this point, reformative training is no longer an appropriate option. It bears reiterating that reformative training lies within the spectrum of rehabilitative sentences (Al-Ansari at [75]; Public Prosecutor v Ong Jack Hong [2016] 5 SLR 166 at [11]; ASR at [134]; see also Sentencing Practice in the Subordinate Courts (LexisNexis, 3rd Ed, 2013) at p 53). Between reformative training and probation, reformative training is the better choice for a young offender for whom rehabilitation remains the principal consideration, but who also needs a measure of deterrence. But where, as in this case, rehabilitation has been displaced as the dominant sentencing consideration, reformative training ceases ordinarily to be a viable option and the appropriate sentences must be the legislatively prescribed options such as imprisonment and caning.”

36     The Court in Boaz, at [30] stated that “the focus on rehabilitation can be diminished or even eclipsed by such considerations as deterrence or retribution where the circumstances warrant. Broadly speaking, this happens in cases where (a) the offence is serious, (b) the harm caused is severe, (c) the offender is hardened and recalcitrant, or (d) the conditions do not exist to make rehabilitative sentencing options such as probation or reformative training viable”.

Rehabilitation had been Displaced as the Dominant Sentencing Principle

37     At the first stage of the Al-Ansari framework., I found that rehabilitation had been displaced as the dominant sentencing consideration in this case.

The Gravity of the Offence

38     The offence of voluntarily causing grievous hurt with a dangerous weapon was an extremely serious one. The punishment prescribed for such an offence under section 326 of the Penal Code was imprisonment for life, or with imprisonment which may extend to 15 years, and the offender may also be liable to fine or to caning.

39     The offence itself aside, the circumstances in which the Accused had committed the grievous hurt were horrifying. He had brandished his 23.5 cm-long-blade bread knife in public, swung it at Praveen’s head the moment he saw him and used it to slash Praveen’s hand. With knife in hand, the Accused gave chase when Praveen attempted to flee and he continued to use the knife to slash Praveen on his upper torso and hands. The Accused was relentless in his attack as he continued to pursue Praveen, and along with Niswan (the co-accused), they slashed his head and torso again. As mentioned in the Statement of Facts[note: 14], there was a total of three separate locations in the vicinity of Block 175 Boon Lay Drive that Praveen was attacked, resulting in multiple slash wounds. These were near the carpark, outside a coffeeshop and also a walkway near a main road, in a residential neighbourhood, in broad daylight. The use of violence by the Accused was both blatant[note: 15] and brazen[note: 16].

The Culpability of the Accused

40     The manner in which the Accused had planned the attack was also highly premeditated. The Accused was aware that Praveen would be attending the wedding of a mutual friend, Emmanuel on 6 April 2022. He enlisted the help of the co-accused Niswan in the attack and deliberately purchased two bread knives for use in the attack[note: 17]. On the day in question, the Accused and Niswan concealed the knives in the waistband of their pants, tracked Praveen’s movements through his social media account and laid in wait for Praveen in the vicinity of Block 175 Boon Lay Drive. This was not an attack on a victim which arose from spontaneous events or an event which happened on the spur of the moment, but one which was carefully planned and executed by the Accused. The Accused’s level of culpability was high.

The Severity of the Harm Caused

41     The vicious nature of the Accused’s attacks, in common intention with Niswan, was manifested by the extent of injury suffered by Praveen. The medical reports[note: 18] documented the severity of harm caused to Praveen, which included three fractures and multiple lacerations all over his body. He underwent several surgeries and was hospitalized for about 10 days, with hospitalization leave for 118 days (including his hospitalization).

42     Bearing the circumstances of this case in mind, I agreed with the Prosecution that there was a strong need for deterrence. There was much public disquiet as the Accused’s violent acts which took place in an HDB[note: 19] estate in broad daylight, were captured on video and circulated widely online. Prosecution submitted that offences of this type created deep public disquiet and deterrent sentences were necessary to prevent the proliferation of such offences. There was also a need to send a message to future youthful offenders in public who act similarly. I shared the view that a strong deterrent sentence was necessary to signal the Courts’ disapprobation of the conduct of the Accused[note: 20].

The Accused’s Antecedents

43     Whilst I was constantly mindful that the Accused was 20 years old when he committed this offence (and there would ordinarily be a presumptive emphasis on rehabilitation), I noted from his antecedents that this young man has had several opportunities for rehabilitation.

44     Prior to the offence, the Accused had been placed on probation twice. On 27 September 2017, when he was only 15 years old, the Accused was ordered to undergo 21 months’ probation for two counts of rioting under section 147 of the Penal Code. On 3 June 2020, the Accused was again ordered to undergo probation for 21 months, for being a member of an unlawful assembly under section 143 of the Penal Code. An additional charge of rioting was taken into consideration for purposes of sentencing on that occasion.

45     The Accused’s history of offending did not start at 15 years old. As pointed out by the Prosecution, at the tender age of 12, the Accused was already involved in a fight outside school where he was issued a police warning. At 14 years old, he committed an offence of wilful trespass and was given a 12-month conditional warning and placed on the Guidance Programme. Thereafter, as his antecedents reflect, the Accused was placed on two rounds of probation. Even whilst on probation, he received several verbal, written and court warnings for breaching time restriction. On one of these occasions, he committed a fresh offence of rioting. On his second term of probation (for rioting and for being a member of an unlawful assembly), the Accused again failed to abide by his time restriction and was involved in a fight in February 2021, for which he was given a warning[note: 21].

46     Subsequent to the second order of probation, the Accused went on to commit an offence under section 323 of the Penal Code - that of causing hurt – which formed the subject matter of one of the charges now taken into consideration (MAC 905948/2022). Whilst he was on police bail for the TIC offence, he committed this current offence of voluntarily causing grievous hurt with dangerous weapon, against two victims – Praveen and Saran. After being charged in court and released on bail, the Accused breached his bail conditions. At that point, bail was no longer offered to the Accused[note: 22]. Accused was in remand when he pleaded guilty to the charges before this court. The Accused’s history of offending and persistent disregard of the law plainly showed him to be recalcitrant.

Deterrence was the Dominant Sentencing Principle

47     Taking into account all the circumstances of this case, in my final determination at the first stage of the Al-Ansari framework, I was satisfied that deterrence had displaced rehabilitation as the dominant sentencing consideration. Since rehabilitation was not the dominant sentencing consideration, reformative training, which in essence is a rehabilitative sentence, was no longer an appropriate sentencing option.

48     In proposing the framework in Al-Ansari, the Court stated at [77], that “if the offence was particularly heinous or the offender has a long history of offending, then reform and rehabilitation may not even be possible or relevant, notwithstanding the youth of the offender. In this case, the statutorily prescribed punishment (in most cases a term of imprisonment) will be appropriate”. This was one such case.

49     The fact that in some precedent cases, courts have held that rehabilitation remains a dominant sentencing consideration despite the gravity of the offence committed by the young offender, was not a compelling argument for this Court to find that rehabilitation remained the main focus of sentencing in this case[note: 23]. The gravity of the offence is only but one aspect of a total and holistic consideration of all circumstances of the case in order to arrive at a final determination of what the dominant sentencing consideration ought to be.

50     In the case of Muhammad Zuhairie Adely Bin Zulkifli (“Adely”) [2016] SGHC 134, a case heavily relied on by Defence Counsel where on appeal the court imposed a term of reformative training in place of imprisonment on an offender convicted of a similar offence under section 326 Penal Code, the circumstances may be distinguished. In Adely, the offender was only 17 years of age at the time of the offence; he was also untraced. As highlighted by the Prosecution, the offender was the lone attacker and there were no injuries on the face of the victim[note: 24].

51     Further and in reference to Adely, the Court of Appeal in Mendel took the opportunity at [14] to clarify the proposition “that at the first stage of the Al-Ansari framework, reformative training may still be an option even for young offenders for whom the primacy of rehabilitation had been provisionally displaced, but who showed a somewhat less exceptional capacity for reform than would be required to qualify for probation”, was not a correct one. It was reiterated that rehabilitation must remain the dominant sentencing consideration at the first stage of the Al-Ansari framework before the court may consider whether probation or reformative training or some other type or combination of community-based sentence would be the correct way to achieve this[note: 25].

52     On the reasons as elaborated above, I was satisfied that deterrence had come to the fore as the dominant sentencing consideration in this case; not rehabilitation. Reformative training was therefore, not a viable sentencing option for the Court.

The Appropriate Sentence

Section 326 of the Penal Code

53     The Prosecution submitted for an imprisonment term of 36 months and six strokes of the cane for the offence under section 326 of the Penal Code, given the serious injuries on the victim, the planned nature of the attack, the effect of the TIC charges and the Accused’s antecedents[note: 26].

54     In my mind, the sentence proposed by the Prosecution was more than fair. The precedents referred to by the Prosecution[note: 27] revealed a sentencing range of 36 months’ imprisonment upwards.

55     Taking the approach in Public Prosecutor v Miya Manik [2020] SGHC 164 (“Miya Manik”), where the Court first considered the indicative sentence if the charge had been under s 325 of the Penal Code before giving an uplift for the nature of the dangerous weapon used, and subsequently adjusting the sentence according to the aggravating and mitigating factors[note: 28], the sentence which the Accused faced would have been much higher than 36 months’ imprisonment.

56     The Court of Appeal in Public Prosecutor v BDB [2017] SGCA 69 (“BDB”) at [56] indicated that in a case where the grievous hurt takes the form of multiple fractures (viz, fractures to the left elbow, left calf, and left 8th to 11th ribs[note: 29]), the indicative starting point should be a term of imprisonment of around three years and six months. The indicative starting point might be higher or lower depending on the type and seriousness of the injuries caused. In view of the injuries Praveen suffered which consisted of lacerations and wounds on his head, face, limbs and fractures on his left hand, left finger and right elbow as well as an extensive hospitalization leave, the indicative starting sentence under section 325 of the Penal Code, would likely have been in excess of three years and six months’ imprisonment, or minimally, not less than three years and six months’ imprisonment. At this first step in Miya Manik, the uplift for the type of weapon used and the aggravating factors had not even featured.

57     That said, I was mindful that the Accused had committed the offence when he was under 21 years old. He had surrendered himself to the authorities and pleaded guilty. Along with caning which I considered necessary to impose given the aggravating nature of the attack and the severe harm caused, I was of the view that the composite sentence of 36 months’ imprisonment and six strokes of the cane would be sufficient for this young man.

Section 14(3) Societies Act

58     As submitted by the Prosecution, the usual sentencing range for such an offence is two to three months’ imprisonment. The Accused was a member of ‘Ang Soon Tong’ Triad Society from 1 July 2020 until his date of arrest for the offence under section 326 of the Penal Code. He was a fighter in the organization[note: 30]. I was of the view that a sentence of three months’ imprisonment would be appropriate.

Total Sentence

59     I ordered both the imprisonment sentences to run concurrently, making it a total of 36 months’ imprisonment and six strokes of the cane. I was satisfied that the sentence was not manifestly excessive and was proportionate to the Accused’s culpability and criminality in this case.

60     The co-accused in this case, Niswan, pleaded guilty to similar charges as the Accused - under section 326 of the Penal Code and section 14(3) of the Societies Act – along with one other charge of criminal intimidation under section 506 of the Penal Code and three other traffic-related offences[note: 31]. As with the Accused, I did not consider reformative training a viable sentencing option and I proceeded to impose the same sentence on each of the charges for Niswan.

Conclusion

61     The sentence was backdated to 7 April 2022 when the Accused was arrested. I further directed the prisons to take into account the periods of remand and to exclude the bail period[note: 32]. Accused was remanded at the time of his guilty plea and when sentence was passed.

62     The Accused is currently serving the sentence.
